Characterizing the degradation of apatite waste form material (Experimental)

    This project is to characterize the mechanism of iodine release from iodoapatite Pb9.85(VO4)6I1.7, a promising waste form to immobilize radionuclide iodine-129, in aqueous environments. I conducted semi-dynamic leaching tests (ASTM C1308) on bulk samples in cap-sealed Teflon vessels filled with aqueous solutions, imitating various environmental conditions. The collected aliquots were analyzed by ICP-MS and ICP-OES, and the chemical and physical alterations to samples were characterized by XRD, SEM-EDS, TEM microscopy, Raman, and infrared spectroscopy. Solution and surface characterization showed that the iodine release is controlled by both short-term diffusion and long-term congruent dissolution processes, and the release rate is highly sensitive to the solution chemistry such as solution pH, ionic species, and saturation state. In addition, I observed formation of secondary phases, such as Pb2V2O7 from pH 4, Pb5(VO4)3Cl from NaCl, and Pb5(VO4)3OH from pH 10. The findings of this project provide a theoretical framework for improving long-term disposal safety of nuclear waste. I made an award-wining video on nuclear waste. Check out my video.

Free energy calculation of fluid-rock interactions at oil-shale interfaces (Computational)

    My second project uses computational modeling to study the oil-rock interactions in shale nanopores. Strong surface effect at nanoscale makes it challenging to conduct experimental investigation in nanopore. The heterogeneous nature of hydrocarbon system further complicates the phenomena in shale nanopore. To gain mechanistic insights into oil-shale interactions in nanopore, I applied Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations to calculate the Gibbs free energy of the light oil interactions with both hydrophilic calcite and porous kerogen surfaces at nanoscale. Free energy profiles in the figures above illustrate how the system energies change as a function of the distance between oil compounds and surfaces. The value of plateau is the total energy required for desorption. The effects of oil polarity (octane vs octanthiol), oil clustering (single molecule vs 30-molecule cluster), water (presence vs absence), and temperature were investigated. Based on the energy difference, I was able to determine favorable conditions to promote the desorption of oil in shale nanopore. This project demonstrates that MD technique can provide energetic insight into fluid-rock interactions, which leads to important implications for oil recovery in unconventional shale development.
